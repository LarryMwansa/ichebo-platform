
## My Thought On Onboarding


Induction must be understood as the true starting point of the formation process. The most important principle is that before anyone reaches Level One, they must first pass through Level Zero, because Level Zero is where induction belongs.

Induction should not be placed inside Level One itself. Instead, it must exist as a distinct foundational stage within Level Zero. This stage prepares people to participate properly within the Kingdom Governance System before they move into formal training and service.

There are two primary groups of people who enter through induction.

The first group consists of existing believers—people who may already be part of church life or have been around the system for some time. These individuals still require reorientation so they can clearly understand how they fit within the Kingdom and, more specifically, how they fit within the Kingdom Governance System.

The second group consists of those who are kingdom-curious—people who are drawn to the Kingdom and desire to become part of it. They also need preparation and guidance so they can be introduced properly into the life, values, and expectations of the Kingdom.

At this stage, other important factors must also be considered, such as gifting, skills, occupation, and personal capacity. These elements help determine how individuals may eventually function within the system and where they may best serve. While this still requires further refinement, it is an important part of the induction process.

Once induction is complete, individuals can then proceed into Level One training and the formal development pathway. Induction also prepares people for proper placement within their respective communities. No one should be placed into a community until they have first completed induction.

For this reason, induction must function as a structured onboarding environment that brings everyone to the same foundational level.

I believe there should be a dedicated Induction Tenant—a specific onboarding community where all new entrants are first placed. This functions almost like the “outer court of the Gentiles,” a preparatory space where people are received, oriented, and formed before being assigned to their long-term communities.

Only after completing this induction stage should individuals be sorted according to their geographical location and integrated into their respective tenants.

It is also important to remember that these tenants may exist both as digital twins and as organic, brick-and-mortar communities in physical locations. Both dimensions must be considered in the design of the induction system.

My thinking is that individuals should remain within the Induction Tenant for a period of twelve weeks. During these twelve weeks, the full induction programme should be carried out, covering orientation, preparation, reorientation, foundational teachings, and initial assessment of gifting, skills, and placement.

This twelve-week period forms the complete Level Zero process.

After successfully completing these first twelve weeks, individuals can then transition into the next stage, which becomes the second half of the formation journey—Level One Training.

In this way, Level One should be understood in two parts: the first half is the induction process within Level Zero, and the second half is the formal Level One training itself.

This creates a clearer progression where induction is not confused with training, but serves as the necessary foundation before deeper formation, service preparation, and community placement can begin.
