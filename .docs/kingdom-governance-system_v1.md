# 2.1 The Kingdom Mandate Framework

## 2.1.1 Foundational Declaration

At the heart of the Christian calling lies a mandate that defines our purpose in the world.

This mandate is not a human invention, nor a modern programme of religious activity. It originates from the final instructions given by **Jesus Christ** to His apostles after His resurrection, as recorded in **Acts of the Apostles 1:1-8**.

After His suffering, death, and resurrection, Christ appeared to His disciples over a period of forty days. During this time He gave them convincing proofs that He was alive and spoke to them about the Kingdom of God. These teachings were not simply reflections on spiritual matters, they were preparation for a mission that would shape the course of history.

Before His ascension, Christ gave His disciples a clear instruction.

They were to wait for the promise of the Father—the coming of the Holy Spirit—through whom they would receive power. With that power they would become witnesses of Christ, beginning in Jerusalem, extending through Judea and Samaria, and ultimately reaching the ends of the earth.

This instruction established the pattern for the work of the Church across all generations.

It is what we refer to as **the Kingdom Mandate**.

---

## 2.1.2 The Nature of the Mandate

The Kingdom Mandate calls believers to **occupy and influence the world for the purposes of God (Luke 19:13 KJV)**.

This mandate is not fulfilled through withdrawal from society, nor through passive observance of the world around us. Instead, it requires active engagement with the structures, institutions, and communities that shape human life.

The Kingdom of God advances when the people of God carry the character, authority, and influence of Christ into every sphere of life. (Matthew 5:13)

This influence operates through three interconnected dimensions:

- **Influence**
    
- **The Spectre of Righteousness**
    
- **Our Work**
    

Together, these dimensions form the practical expression of the Kingdom Mandate.

---

## 2.2.3 Influence as Kingdom Authority

The Kingdom of God does not advance through domination by force. It advances through **transformative influence**.

The authority of our influence does not come from human power, but from our alignment with Christ. As representatives of our Master, we carry the weight of His character and the example of His life into the world.

There is an important distinction between **peace-keeping** and **passivity**.

Peace-keeping is an active responsibility. It involves guiding, reconciling, and encouraging others toward righteousness. Those who keep peace participate in restoring relationships and establishing justice within society.

Passivity, however, withdraws from responsibility and avoids engagement. That is not the posture of the Kingdom.

The Kingdom Mandate calls us to influence the world—not to dominate people, but to guide society toward righteousness.

---

## 2.1.4 The Spectre of Righteousness

Our Master governs through what can be described as the **Spectre of Righteousness (Psalm 45:6 (NKJV))**.

This means that His authority is manifested through righteousness, through the visible expression of truth, justice, and integrity in the lives of His people.

The rule of Christ becomes evident when righteousness is practiced in three areas:

- **In our hearts**, through personal transformation and obedience to God.
    
- **In our work**, through integrity, excellence, and faithful stewardship.
    
- **In society**, through our responsibility to influence communities and institutions.
    

In this way the Kingdom becomes visible, not merely as a spiritual concept, but as a living reality expressed through the lives of those who belong to it.

---

## 2.1.5 The Fruit of Our Work

The grace we receive through Christ is like a seed planted within the human heart.

This seed is not meant to remain dormant. It grows, develops, and eventually produces fruit.

That fruit is expressed through the work entrusted to each person.

Our work—whether in ministry, service, leadership, education, business, governance, or community life—becomes a channel through which the Kingdom of God touches society.

Through our work we influence the world.

Through our labour we serve our communities.

Through the fruit of our calling we provide nourishment, guidance, and transformation for society.

Every person therefore carries a responsibility to act within their sphere of influence with actions rooted in righteousness.

---

## 2.1.6 The Scope of the Mandate

The Kingdom Mandate cannot be fulfilled in isolation.

It is not accomplished by remaining confined within religious institutions or separated from the realities of society. The people of God are called to engage the world, not withdraw from it.

Christ Himself established the pattern of Kingdom expansion.

The influence of the Kingdom begins at specific epicentres and spreads outward. It moves from Jerusalem to Judea, to Samaria, and ultimately to the ends of the earth.

In geographical terms, the Kingdom spreads from centres of influence until it reaches the whole world.

In terms of time, the Kingdom transcends generations. Its influence must shape the past, guide the present, and prepare the future.

This is the scope of the mandate entrusted to the Church.

---

1.  **Kingdom Mandate**
2.  **Apostolic Governance (The Agency)**
3.  **Networks and Church Collectives**
4.  **Sceptre Communities (Church Nodes)**
5.  **Individual Disciples**

This structure ensures that authority flows from the mandate to the community while maintaining spiritual alignment and mission focus.

---

It does not belong to an organisation but to **Christ's commission to His Church**.

It provides the **spiritual authority, mission, and theological foundation** for everything below it.

**Functions**

- Defines the purpose of the Church on earth
    
- Guides the long-term direction of the movement
    
- Frames the governance philosophy

## 2.1.8 Preparing for the Work Ahead

Yet throughout history, the fulfilment of this mandate has often been hindered by various challenges—both internal and external.

If we are to effectively carry out the work entrusted to us, we must first understand the obstacles that prevent the people of God from fully realising their mandate.

Recognising and addressing these hindrances is essential if the Kingdom Mandate is to be expressed in its fullness.

The following section therefore examines the **Kingdom Mandate Hindrances**—the barriers that must be identified and overcome in order for the work of the Kingdom to advance.

# 2.2.0 Kingdom Mandate Hindrances

## 2.2.1 Barriers to Kingdom Effectiveness

The Kingdom Mandate calls the people of God to participate actively in the advancement of God’s purposes in the earth. This mandate is not limited to spiritual devotion or ecclesiastical life alone, but encompasses the broader responsibility of representing the values, authority, and character of the Kingdom of God across every sphere of society. From the family and community to economic, cultural, and national life, believers are entrusted with the responsibility of bearing witness to the Kingdom through both proclamation and practice.

However, throughout history and particularly in the contemporary context, the effectiveness of this mandate has often been limited by a number of structural, spiritual, and operational challenges. These challenges do not necessarily arise from a lack of sincere faith or commitment, but rather from misalignments in vision, deficiencies in organization and capability, and difficulties in discerning and responding to the changing seasons of God’s work in the world.

Within the framework of Kingdom governance, these challenges can be understood as **hindrances that obstruct the full realization of the Kingdom Mandate**. When such hindrances are present, the capacity of Kingdom communities to mobilize effectively, influence society, and steward their calling is significantly weakened. As a result, the transformative potential of the Kingdom may remain largely unrealized despite the presence of vibrant religious activity.

It is therefore necessary to examine these barriers with clarity and honesty. Identifying these hindrances is not intended as criticism of the Church or of Kingdom communities, but rather as a step toward greater alignment with the purposes of God. Through understanding these limitations, it becomes possible to develop more effective structures, cultivate stronger capabilities, and foster a more faithful response to the mission entrusted to the people of God.

The following sections outline **six key hindrances that affect the effectiveness of the Kingdom Mandate**. These barriers emerge across several levels of Kingdom governance, including vision alignment, strategic discernment, organizational mobilization, capability development, and societal engagement. Together, they reveal the areas where renewal, reform, and realignment are necessary for the Kingdom community to function more effectively in the present age.

The six hindrances explored in this framework are:

1. **Misalignment of Vision and Mandate**
    
2. **Failure to Understand the Times and Prepare for the Next Phase**
    
3. **Lack of Adaptability and Spiritual Agility**
    
4. **Deficiencies in Kingdom Mobilization**
    
5. **Deficiencies in Capabilities and Skills**
    
6. **Decline in Civic Engagement**
    

By examining these barriers, the Kingdom Governance System seeks to provide insight into the structural and spiritual adjustments required for the Kingdom community to operate with greater coherence, readiness, and impact in fulfilling its mandate in the world.


---

## 2.2.2 Misalignment of Vision and Mandate

Another important hindrance to the fulfilment of the Kingdom Mandate is the **misalignment between God’s vision and our own expectations of what that vision should be**.

This challenge can be seen clearly in the interaction between the disciples and the Lord Jesus just before His ascension. In Acts of the Apostles 1:6–8, the apostles ask Jesus whether He will restore the kingdom to Israel at that time. Their question reveals that they were still interpreting God’s purposes through their own expectations—particularly a political or national restoration of Israel.

Jesus redirects their understanding. Instead of focusing on the timing of political restoration, He tells them that such matters are in the authority of the Father. Rather than answering their expectation directly, He gives them their true mandate: they will receive power through the Holy Spirit and become His witnesses, beginning in Jerusalem and extending outward to Judea, Samaria, and to the ends of the earth.

This exchange illustrates a recurring problem that believers and faith communities often face. There is a tendency to **take the vision of God and attempt to fit it into our own frameworks, assumptions, and expectations**. Instead of seeking to understand what God is presently doing, we sometimes assume that His work must conform to what we believe He should be doing or where we believe His focus should lie.

In many cases, this misalignment arises from looking primarily at **how God worked in the past** and assuming that the same patterns must define His present activity. While the history of God’s work is instructive, it does not always determine the exact way in which He will move in every new season.

Another dimension of this misalignment is the subtle tendency to **use God’s platform to advance our own agendas**. Rather than faithfully carrying out the work entrusted to us, we sometimes attempt to shape God’s mission around our own plans, ambitions, or institutional goals. In such situations, the work that is being pursued may appear to be the work of God, but in reality it may be driven more by human intentions than by divine direction.

The Kingdom Mandate requires a posture of **alignment rather than assumption**. It calls for humility, discernment, and obedience—seeking to understand what God is truly doing and faithfully participating in that work rather than redefining it according to our preferences.

Where vision becomes misaligned, the mission of the Kingdom can easily lose its clarity and direction. Therefore, continual realignment with God’s purposes is essential for the effective fulfilment of the Kingdom Mandate.


---

## 2.2.3 Understanding the Times and Preparing for the Next Phase

Another important hindrance to the fulfilment of the Kingdom Mandate is the **failure to properly understand the times in which we are living**. Discernment of the times is essential for knowing how the people of God should respond to the purposes of God in any given season.

The Lord Jesus once challenged the people of His day for their inability to recognize the spiritual moment they were living in. In Gospel of Matthew 16:2–3, He observed that while people were able to interpret natural signs in the sky to predict changes in the weather, they struggled to discern the **signs of the times** related to God’s unfolding work.

This same challenge continues to confront believers today. In the present moment, there is a growing sense that the Church and the wider Kingdom community are living in a **time of transition**. The current season appears to involve shifts in structures, priorities, and approaches as God’s purposes move forward into a new phase.

Recognizing such transitions is the first step. Kingdom communities must be able to **discern when a season is changing** and understand what that change requires of them. Without this awareness, people may continue operating according to patterns that belonged to a previous season while missing the opportunities of the present one.

Understanding the times also requires **intentional preparation for what lies ahead**. As new phases emerge, believers must develop the skills, knowledge, and spiritual maturity necessary to function effectively in what God is bringing about. Many individuals are being called to step into new responsibilities and roles within this unfolding season.

Because of this, **roles and structures may change**. What has previously been the established order may no longer function in the same way. Such changes should not necessarily be interpreted as loss, demotion, or displacement. Rather, they can represent the natural evolution of responsibilities as the Kingdom advances into new territory.

It is therefore important to **sensitize existing leadership and communities** to these transitions so that they can understand and embrace the changes taking place. When people recognize that shifting roles are part of a larger Kingdom progression, they are more able to participate constructively in the transition rather than resist it.

The biblical example of Jonah and his mission to Nineveh illustrates the kind of engagement that may be required when God is calling for transformation. Jonah’s message of repentance reached every level of society—from the ordinary citizens to the highest leadership—demonstrating how God’s call for renewal can extend across the entire social order.

In a similar way, understanding the times today calls for a **broad engagement with every level of society**. Transformation must reach families, communities, economic structures, cultural institutions, and national life. The work of the Kingdom is not confined to isolated spiritual spaces but seeks to bring renewal to the whole fabric of society.

Preparing for the next phase of the Kingdom Mandate therefore requires both **discernment and readiness**: discernment to recognize the season that God is unfolding, and readiness to step into the roles and responsibilities required to carry His purposes forward into the next stage.

---

## 2.2.4 Adaptability, Rigidity, and the Need for Agility in the Kingdom Mandate

One of the major hindrances behind the fulfillment of the Kingdom Mandate is the **lack of adaptability**. This challenge can be viewed from several important perspectives.

First, there is the matter of **following the movement and direction of the Lord**. As believers, we acknowledge that it is the Lord whom we follow. He is the one who sets the pace for what He desires to accomplish in any given time. Because of this, it becomes essential for us to discern the **season of His word**, understand His intentions, and perceive the motivations behind what He is seeking to achieve.

Our responsibility is therefore to **align ourselves with His direction**, bending our will toward His purposes rather than assuming we already know them. Unfortunately, there is often a tendency among us to believe that we already know what ought to be done or what is in the mind of God. In reality, this assumption can lead to rigidity and resistance to the fresh leading of the Spirit. True kingdom effectiveness requires a posture of humility and adaptability.

Another area where this lack of adaptability is evident is in the **mobilization of kingdom communities, particularly within churches**. Many churches have become rigid in their responses to the changing environment around them. They struggle to adapt to **social realities, cultural shifts, and ideological changes** that are taking place in society.

Instead of engaging with these shifts thoughtfully and strategically, there is often a tendency to withdraw. This can create a **hermetic or insular posture**, where communities isolate themselves rather than interacting with the world they are called to influence. While such withdrawal may sometimes feel like a way of coping with external pressures, it ultimately limits meaningful engagement and impact.

This lack of engagement does not only affect the church's interaction with society. It can also be seen at **more intimate levels, including within the home and other relational sp aces**. Where adaptability and engagement are absent, meaningful dialogue and influence are also diminished.

This challenge brings to mind the teaching of the Lord Jesus Christ regarding **new wine and new wineskins**. The Lord emphasized that new wine must be placed into new wineskins. This imagery speaks to the necessity of **elasticity and capacity for change** within us.

Just as new wineskins are able to stretch and expand as the wine ferments and matures, believers and kingdom communities must develop the **spiritual flexibility and readiness to grow**. Without this elasticity, the vessel cannot contain what God is pouring out.

Therefore, fulfilling the Kingdom Mandate requires a willingness to **renew our structures, our approaches, and even our thinking** so that we can adequately receive and steward what God is doing in each season.


---

## 2.2.5 Deficiencies in Kingdom Mobilization

Another significant hindrance to the fulfillment of the Kingdom Mandate is the **deficiency in how the Kingdom is mobilized in our time**. In the present context, churches—often understood as key units within the Kingdom community—play an important role in organizing and mobilizing believers. However, the way this mobilization is structured today sometimes reveals serious limitations.

One of the major challenges lies in the presence of **institutional biases** within church structures. Over time, many churches have developed systems that prioritize the survival and expansion of their own institutions rather than the broader advancement of the Kingdom of God. As a result, what was originally meant to serve the Kingdom mission can gradually become centered on maintaining the organization itself.

This tendency has contributed to the emergence of **silos or monolithic structures** within the Christian landscape. Individual churches or ministries often function as isolated entities, focused primarily on their own internal communities. Leadership structures in such settings may become highly centralized, frequently revolving around a single leader and a small leadership circle. While these structures may provide organizational stability, they can also limit broader collaboration and shared mission.

In some cases, even when churches are part of networks or associations, they still operate in ways that are **insular and self-protective**. Membership and participation may be restricted to those who fully align with the internal culture or directives of the organization. This can unintentionally create an environment where the institution’s priorities take precedence over the collective mission of the Kingdom.

When organizations become overly focused on preserving their structures, resources, and influence, the result can be a form of **institutional self-preservation**. Instead of mobilizing believers outward into society to influence and transform communities, energy is directed inward toward sustaining the institution. In this sense, the mobilization that occurs may serve the growth of the organization rather than the broader mission of the Kingdom.

Another related challenge is **fragmentation and tribalism** within the Christian community. Churches and ministries often operate like separate camps, with limited cooperation between them. This fragmentation weakens the collective witness of believers and reduces the potential impact that could arise from unified action. Yet the teaching of the Lord Jesus consistently emphasized love, unity, and mutual recognition among His followers.

In contrast, when communities of believers recognize one another as part of the same Kingdom family and are able to work together, their influence within society becomes significantly stronger. Collaboration allows the Kingdom message and its values to spread more effectively across communities, cultures, and institutions.

The current pattern of isolated, institutional expansion often results in the spread of **organizational Christianity rather than the broader influence of the Kingdom itself**. While institutions may grow, the transformative presence of the Kingdom in society may remain limited.

A potential corrective can be found in the **pattern of the early church**, which demonstrated a more decentralized and relational form of organization. Early Christian communities recognized themselves as part of a wider fellowship of believers and worked cooperatively in advancing the mission entrusted to them.

Recovering aspects of this approach could help break down the silos that currently exist. By fostering collaboration, mutual recognition, and shared mission among believers and communities of faith, the Church can move closer to a model of **Kingdom-centered mobilization**.

Ultimately, strengthening Kingdom mobilization requires a shift from institutional self-focus toward **collective Kingdom readiness**—where believers and communities see themselves not as isolated entities, but as interconnected participants in a larger mission to influence and transform the world.

## 2.2.6 Deficiencies in Capabilities and Skills

Another major hindrance to the fulfillment of the Kingdom Mandate is the **lack of the necessary capabilities and skills required to function effectively as subjects of the Kingdom**.

Believers can be understood as participants in what may be called **Kingdom service or Kingdom stewardship**. In many ways, the people of the Kingdom operate as a unique kind of community within the world. In contemporary terms, they function as a **hybrid people**—individuals who live within the natural structures of society while also carrying a spiritual identity rooted in their new life in Christ.

This means that Kingdom citizens operate with **two important dimensions**. On the one hand, they participate in the normal human structures of society—work, education, governance, and social life. On the other hand, they possess a spiritual nature that comes from being **born again and born of the Spirit**. It is this spiritual dimension that should shape their character, motivations, discernment, and effectiveness in fulfilling the purposes of the Kingdom.

However, one of the central challenges is that this spiritual dimension is often **underdeveloped or immature**. Many believers experience spiritual transformation, but the growth and formation required to mature in that new identity does not always take place. As a result, the aspect of their lives that should empower them to function effectively as Kingdom representatives remains weak or undernourished.

A significant reason for this deficiency is the **lack of structured frameworks for training and development**. In many contexts, there are limited systems designed to intentionally equip believers with the spiritual disciplines, leadership abilities, and practical competencies required for Kingdom work. Without such formation, individuals may remain spiritually inexperienced or uncertain about how to translate their faith into meaningful influence in the world.

This gap in development affects both **leaders and followers alike**. Leaders may lack the depth of formation needed to guide others effectively, while those who follow them may also lack the training and guidance required to grow into mature Kingdom participants. When both leadership and community formation are weak, the overall capacity of the Kingdom community to function effectively is significantly diminished.

The consequences of this are profound. Without the necessary skills, formation, and spiritual maturity, believers struggle to live, lead, and serve in ways that fully reflect the values and purposes of the Kingdom. The potential for meaningful impact—whether in communities, institutions, or broader society—remains largely unrealized.

Addressing this challenge requires intentional efforts to **develop believers in both spiritual formation and practical competence**. When individuals are equipped with the right capabilities, they are better able to embody Kingdom values, exercise wise leadership, and contribute meaningfully to the advancement of the Kingdom in every sphere of life.

## 2.2.7 Civic Engagement as a Challenge to the Kingdom Mandate

Another significant hindrance to the fulfilment of the Kingdom Mandate is the **decline in civic or societal engagement**. This refers to our engagement with the broader society beyond the walls of our faith communities. Scripture calls believers to be **“the salt of the earth,”** meaning that wherever we are present, we should bring preservation, life, and transformation to that environment.

However, there has been a noticeable reduction in this kind of engagement. In many cases, believers and faith communities have withdrawn from active participation in societal life, choosing instead a posture of separation or disengagement. Several factors contribute to this trend.

One factor is what may be described as a **hypocritical posture** that stems from an internal spiritual weakness. When there is a form of spiritual bankruptcy within us—when the vitality and authenticity of our faith are diminished—we lose the ability to shape the world around us. In biblical terms, it is like **salt that has lost its saltiness**. When salt no longer has its preserving and seasoning power, it cannot influence the environment around it. In the same way, when something within us has become lifeless or compromised, our capacity to impact society meaningfully is weakened.

Another contributing factor is the tendency toward **isolation from the world**, often justified as a means of maintaining moral purity. This attitude can resemble the example seen in the parable of the **Good Samaritan**, where the priest avoided engagement with the wounded man on the road. The concern for maintaining religious or moral cleanliness can sometimes lead to avoiding the very places where compassion, justice, and witness are most needed. When believers withdraw in this way, they abandon the spaces where the Kingdom’s influence should be most visible.

There is also the issue of misunderstanding concepts such as the **separation of church and state**. While it is important to recognize that the Kingdom of God operates with its own identity and authority, this principle should not be interpreted as a justification for disengagement from public life. The church does not surrender its identity when it participates in society; rather, it expresses its Kingdom values within the social and civic spaces it inhabits.

At times, past examples of **extremism or unhealthy alliances between religious institutions and political power** have also caused people to retreat from civic engagement altogether. However, the excesses of the past should not become reasons for complete withdrawal. Instead, they should guide us toward wiser, more principled forms of engagement.

Ultimately, the mission of the Kingdom requires **a visible and active witness within society**. To bear witness means bringing the light of righteousness, justice, and truth into every sphere of life. Our conduct, values, and actions must demonstrate the character of the Kingdom in practical ways.

For the Kingdom Mandate to be fulfilled, believers must therefore rediscover their responsibility to **engage society faithfully and responsibly**, ensuring that the influence of the Kingdom is present in the places where decisions are made, cultures are shaped, and communities are formed.

## 2.2.8 Moving from Hindrances to Kingdom Governance

The hindrances outlined above reveal that the limitations facing the Kingdom Mandate are not merely isolated problems but are **systemic in nature**. They affect multiple layers of Kingdom life, including vision, discernment, adaptability, organizational structure, capability development, and engagement with society. When these barriers exist simultaneously, they weaken the ability of Kingdom communities to function cohesively and to exert meaningful influence in the world.

Recognizing these hindrances is therefore an essential first step. However, diagnosis alone is not sufficient. What is required is a deliberate movement toward **restoration, alignment, and the development of systems that enable Kingdom effectiveness**. This is where the concept of **Kingdom Governance** becomes critical.

Kingdom Governance provides the framework through which the people of God can organize, mobilize, and steward their collective calling in a coherent and sustainable way. Rather than relying solely on informal structures or isolated institutional efforts, Kingdom Governance seeks to cultivate **aligned vision, coordinated action, and shared responsibility** across communities of believers.

Within this perspective, governance does not refer merely to authority structures or institutional administration. Instead, it encompasses the broader processes through which the Kingdom community discerns God’s purposes, equips its members, organizes its efforts, and engages society with wisdom and integrity.

Addressing the hindrances identified earlier therefore requires the development of **governance systems that strengthen alignment, encourage collaboration, cultivate capabilities, and enable believers to function effectively within the changing seasons of God’s work**. Such systems help move the Kingdom community from fragmentation toward unity of purpose, and from reactive activity toward intentional and strategic mission.

The sections that follow will therefore explore the **principles, structures, and operational components of the Kingdom Governance System**. These elements are intended to provide practical pathways through which Kingdom communities can overcome the hindrances described above and more effectively fulfill their mandate in the present age.

# 4.2 Agency Operations Framework

## 4.2.1 The Agency 

The Agency functions as the governing instrument responsible for stewarding the Kingdom mandate and coordinating the mission of the movement.

In order to facilitate the **Kingdom Mandate** and its operational framework, we propose the formation of **The Agency**, which acts as the Kingdom of God’s delegation tasked with developing and implementing this mandate.

The Agency exists to coordinate **personnel, resources, and operations** necessary for advancing the work of the Kingdom. It serves as the organisational instrument through which the Lord’s mandate is translated into structured action within communities, institutions, and society.

At its core, the Agency is designed to function according to what we refer to as the **Acts 6 Protocol** (Acts 6:1–7), which provides a model for balancing spiritual oversight with operational service. In this model, leadership ensures that the mission of Christ advances effectively by organizing people, structures, and responsibilities in a way that preserves both spiritual direction and practical service.

Through this protocol, the Agency operates along two complementary directions of service:

### 4.2.1.1 Service Towards the Master

This dimension performs the **prophetic and apostolic functions** of the movement. Its responsibility is to discern the strategic objectives of the Lord, provide spiritual direction, and ensure that the work of the movement remains aligned with the will and purposes of Christ.

### 4.2.1.2 Service Towards the People

This dimension performs the **implementation and mobilization functions** of the movement. It focuses on organising believers, communities, and operational structures that carry out the mandate in everyday life and in the wider society.

Through the interaction of these two dimensions, the Agency seeks to faithfully advance the work of Christ while effectively serving the communities in which the Kingdom mandate is being expressed.

The work of the Agency is organised through several **Core Institutional Components**. These components serve as the management instruments through which the mission of the Agency is coordinated and implemented.

The Executive Team provides both **spiritual and administrative leadership**, ensuring that the work of the Agency remains aligned with the Kingdom vision while also maintaining effective organisational governance. It coordinates the strategic direction of the movement and ensures that the various branches of the Agency operate in unity toward the fulfilment of the mandate.

The **Disciple Ministry Network (DMN)** forms the relational and operational network of the Agency’s delegates. It brings together individuals and ministries that serve as active participants in advancing the Kingdom mandate.

Through the DMN, believers are connected into a collaborative structure where they can contribute their gifts, ministries, and callings toward the larger mission.

The DMN consists of several specialised branches that facilitate different aspects of Kingdom service.

### 4.2.4.1 Allied Prophetic and Apostolic Services (APAS)

The **Allied Prophetic and Apostolic Services (APAS)** branch is responsible for the spiritual direction and establishment of the ministry according to the guidance of the Lord.

This branch provides apostolic and prophetic oversight to the movement, ensuring that the mission remains aligned with the purposes and leading of Christ. It facilitates discernment, spiritual governance, and strategic direction for the Agency.

This branch operates according to the **APAS Framework**, which governs how prophetic and apostolic functions contribute to the guidance of the movement.

The **Special Envoys Networking Division (SEND)** is responsible for mobilising and connecting believers who serve as delegates of the Agency.

This branch operates according to the **SENDMe Framework**, which governs the mobilisation, coordination, and deployment of Kingdom delegates.

## 4.2.5 Network of Communities (NC)

The **Network of Communities (NC)** represents the decentralised structure through which the Kingdom mandate is implemented at the grassroots level.

It serves as the operational interface between the Agency and the wider society, enabling communities of believers to engage their local environments with the principles and service of the Kingdom.

The Network of Communities is designed specifically to overcome the problem of **institutional silos**, allowing believers to work collaboratively across churches, ministries, and communities in advancing the mission of Christ.

Through this structure, Kingdom mobilisation becomes **distributed, localised, and community-driven**.

---

##  4.2.5.1 The Structure of Sceptre Communities

Within the Network of Communities, the primary operational unit is the **Sceptre Community**.

Sceptre Communities function as **local Church Nodes**, similar to small groups or home-based church cells. They represent the local expression of the Kingdom within a particular community or geographic area.

The aim is to restore the presence of Kingdom community life at the most local level possible, allowing each neighbourhood or district to have a living representation of the Kingdom of God.

Each Sceptre Community is organised by members of that collective and coordinated by a **Community Coordinator**, who acts as the representative of that community within the broader network.

---

##  4.2.5.2 Church Collectives

When **three or more Sceptre Communities** exist within a given geographic demarcation, they may form a **Church Collective**.

A Church Collective represents a cooperative network of Church Nodes operating within a shared area of responsibility, such as wards within a district or districts within a region.

In order to qualify as a Church Collective, the participating communities must demonstrate evidence of active Kingdom work through their contributions to society and the advancement of the mission.

Church Collectives play an important role in the growth of the Network of Communities by supporting new communities and helping establish Kingdom presence in previously uncharted areas.

This organic model of expansion allows the Kingdom network to grow **through multiplication rather than centralisation**.

# **Part IV – Leadership Administration**

## **16. Purpose of Administrative Offices**

The Administrative Offices constitute the **functional leadership structure** through which the Kingdom Governance System is executed, sustained, and advanced.

These Offices exist not as positions of status, but as **stewardship assignments**, entrusted with the responsibility of translating the Kingdom Mandate into coordinated governance, leadership development, ministry operations, and mission expansion.

### **16.1 Core Purpose**

The purpose of Administrative Offices is to:

- provide **structured leadership oversight** across all governance domains
    
- ensure the **faithful implementation** of the Kingdom Mandate
    
- maintain **alignment between doctrine, governance, and operations**
    
- facilitate **coordination across ministries, programmes, and communities**
    
- enable **scalable and sustainable Kingdom growth**
    

### **16.2 Functional Role of Offices**

Each Administrative Office operates as:

- a **governance instrument** (ensuring policy and structure are upheld)
    
- a **leadership centre** (guiding and equipping leaders within its domain)
    
- an **operational hub** (coordinating activities, programmes, and systems)
    
- a **stewardship trust** (managing resources, people, and assignments responsibly)
    

### **16.3 Alignment with Governance Architecture**

Administrative Offices function in direct alignment with:

- the **Seven Governance Pillars** (domain alignment)
    
- the **Agency Operations Framework** (execution structure)
    
- the **Calendars & Programmes Framework** (activity coordination)
    
- the **Formation Layer** (development of people and leaders)
    

Each Office is therefore both:

- **vertical in accountability** (aligned to governance authority), and
    
- **horizontal in collaboration** (integrated across other offices and structures)
    

### **16.4 Non-Hierarchical Stewardship Model**

While Offices carry defined authority, they operate within a **Kingdom stewardship model**, not a secular hierarchy.

This means:

- authority is exercised through **service, responsibility, and accountability**
    
- leadership is validated by **function, fruit, and faithfulness**
    
- Offices collaborate rather than compete
    
- no Office operates in isolation from the broader governance system

## **17. Governance Accountability**

Governance Accountability ensures that all leadership, structures, and operations within the Kingdom Governance System remain **aligned, transparent, faithful, and effective** in fulfilling the Kingdom Mandate.

It establishes the **standards, relationships, and mechanisms** through which authority is exercised responsibly and leadership is held to account.

### **17.1 Principles of Accountability**

All governance accountability operates on the following principles:

- **Spiritual integrity** – alignment with Kingdom values and truth
    
- **Stewardship responsibility** – faithful management of entrusted roles and resources
    
- **Transparency** – openness in decision-making and operations
    
- **Mutual submission** – leadership functioning within relational accountability
    
- **Measurable fruitfulness** – evaluation based on outcomes and impact
    

### **17.2 Accountability Structure**

Accountability flows through the governance system in three primary directions:

#### **1. Upward Accountability (Spiritual & Apostolic Oversight)**

Each Office is accountable to higher governance authority within the Agency, ensuring:

- alignment with the Kingdom Mandate
    
- doctrinal integrity
    
- strategic coherence
    

#### **2. Lateral Accountability (Inter-Office Governance)**

Administrative Offices are accountable to one another through:

- collaborative planning
    
- shared governance processes
    
- integrated programme execution
    

This ensures unity across the governance system.

#### **3. Downward Accountability (Operational & Community Impact)**

Offices are accountable for:

- the effectiveness of programmes and initiatives
    
- leadership development outcomes
    
- the health and growth of communities and networks
    

### **17.3 Accountability Mechanisms**
